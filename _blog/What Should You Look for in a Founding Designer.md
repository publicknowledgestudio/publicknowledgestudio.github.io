At some point, every startup founder asks the same question: do I need a designer? And if so, what kind?

The answer is almost always yes. The harder question is what that looks like in practice. The founding designer — whether a full-time hire, a freelancer, or a studio partnership — shapes how the product looks, how the brand feels, and how the company communicates with the world. Getting this right matters more than most founders realise.

Here is what to look for, and what to avoid.

## In-house vs. studio

There are two paths, and both work.

**Hiring an in-house founding designer** gives you a dedicated team member who grows with the company. They learn the product deeply, build relationships with the engineering team, and are available for the constant stream of small decisions that a startup generates. The downside is that a single designer — no matter how talented — has a limited range. Branding, product design, marketing, pitch decks, illustrations — it is a lot to ask of one person.

**Partnering with a [design studio](https://publicknowledge.co)** gives you a team of specialists from day one, without the commitment of a full-time salary. A good studio brings experience across multiple startups, a refined design process, and the ability to produce a full brand system, website, and pitch deck in a focused engagement. The downside is that the studio is not embedded in your company day-to-day.

Many startups do both. They partner with a studio for the initial brand and product design system, then hire an in-house designer to maintain and extend it. The studio creates the foundation; the in-house designer builds on it.

## Key traits of a great founding designer

Whether you are hiring or choosing a studio, look for these traits:

### A few years of experience, but not too many

You want someone who has shipped real work — products that are live, brands that exist in the world, websites that actual people use. But you do not necessarily want the most senior person you can find. Designers with ten or fifteen years at large companies often struggle with the ambiguity, speed, and resourcefulness that startups demand.

The sweet spot is typically three to six years of experience. Enough to have developed strong craft and judgment, but still hungry, adaptable, and willing to do the unglamorous work that early-stage design requires.

### Well-rounded skills

The founding designer needs to do everything. Brand identity, UI/UX design, pitch deck layout, marketing materials, social media assets, maybe even some basic front-end implementation. Specialists are valuable at later stages, but right now you need a generalist who can move fluidly between different types of design work.

Look for a portfolio that shows range. If every project looks the same, or if the work is all in one category, that is a signal that this person might not be able to handle the variety that a startup demands.

### The ability to take feedback and throw away work

This is critical, and it is the trait that separates good founding designers from bad ones.

At a startup, the design will change. The product will pivot. The positioning will evolve. The founding designer will produce work that looks beautiful but is not working — it does not communicate the right message, it does not convert, it does not fit the direction the company has moved in.

A great founding designer can look at that work, acknowledge that it is not right, and start over without ego. They treat feedback as data, not criticism. They understand that the goal is not to produce impressive-looking design — it is to produce design that works for the business.

Designers who are precious about their work, who resist feedback, or who take revisions personally are a poor fit for the founding designer role.

### Comfort with ambiguity

Startups do not have clear briefs, detailed specifications, or well-defined brand guidelines. The founding designer often has to figure out the brief themselves, based on conversations with the founder, observations about the market, and their own design judgment.

This requires a particular temperament: curiosity, initiative, and the confidence to make decisions with incomplete information. The founding designer should be the kind of person who is energised by ambiguity, not paralysed by it.

### Taste

This one is hard to define and easy to recognise. Look at the portfolio. Does the work feel intentional, cohesive, and considered? Is there a point of view? Do the design decisions feel deliberate, or arbitrary?

Taste is not about following trends. It is about having a design sensibility that produces work which feels right — work that communicates clearly, looks distinctive, and holds up over time.

## What to look for in a design studio

If you go the studio route, here is what matters:

- **Experience with startups.** Agencies that primarily work with large brands have different processes, timelines, and expectations. Look for studios that understand the startup context — lean budgets, fast timelines, evolving requirements.

- **Range in the portfolio.** The studio should be able to handle branding, [startup website design](/blog/design-process-for-startups), product interfaces, and pitch decks. If they only do one thing well, you will need multiple agencies, and the work will not feel cohesive.

- **A lean, collaborative process.** Avoid studios that disappear for four weeks and come back with a big reveal. The best studios work in close collaboration, sharing work early and often, and iterating based on feedback. The process should feel like a partnership, not a transaction.

- **Clear communication.** Timelines, deliverables, pricing, revision rounds — all of this should be transparent upfront. If the studio is vague about what you will get and when, that is a red flag.

## Red flags

Whether hiring or choosing a studio, watch out for these:

- **Only wants to work on "the fun parts."** Design at a startup involves a lot of unsexy work — resizing assets, cleaning up icon sets, making the deck layout consistent across 15 slides. If the designer only wants to do the creative ideation, they are not a good fit for the founding role.

- **Resists feedback.** If the designer gets defensive when you push back on a direction, or treats every revision as a personal affront, this will become a serious problem as the startup evolves and the design needs to change.

- **Has never shipped anything.** A portfolio full of concept work and personal projects is fine for a junior role, but the founding designer needs to have shipped real products. The gap between concept and shipped product is enormous, and you need someone who has crossed it.

- **Treats every project as a personal art piece.** Design at a startup is in service of the business. It needs to communicate, convert, and scale. Designers who prioritise self-expression over business outcomes will produce beautiful work that does not solve the problem.

## The founding designer shapes the company

The founding designer does not just design the product. They shape the visual culture of the company. The standards they set, the systems they build, the taste they bring — all of this persists long after the first version ships.

Choose carefully. Whether it is a [full-time hire or a studio partnership](/blog/when-should-a-startup-invest-in-branding), the founding designer is one of the most consequential early decisions a startup makes.
