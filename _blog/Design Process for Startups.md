Startups are not regular clients. They move fast, they are resource-constrained, and they need design that does multiple jobs at once. A landing page is not just a landing page — it is also a pitch to investors, a recruiting tool, and the first thing a customer sees. A brand identity is not just a logo — it is the system that holds together a pitch deck, a product interface, and a social media presence.

A design process for startups needs to account for all of this. It needs to be lean enough to ship fast, adaptable enough to change direction, and rigorous enough to produce work that actually converts.

Here is how we approach it at [Public Knowledge Studio](https://publicknowledge.co).

## Starting with the story

Every engagement begins with strategy. Before we open Figma, we need to understand the founder's vision, the market context, and the problem being solved.

What is the product? Who is it for? What does the competitive landscape look like? What does the founder want the audience to feel when they encounter this brand for the first time?

From these conversations, a brand narrative emerges. Not a tagline — a narrative. A clear, compelling articulation of what the startup does, why it matters, and who it serves. This narrative becomes the foundation for everything downstream: the visual identity, the website copy, the pitch deck structure, the product onboarding flow.

Getting the story right at the beginning saves enormous amounts of time later. When the narrative is clear, design decisions become easier. When it is not, every deliverable becomes a negotiation.

## Branding that converts

The brand identity is the visual and verbal system that makes a startup recognisable and trustworthy. For early-stage startups, this system needs to work across a wide range of touchpoints from day one: a pitch deck, a landing page, an app interface, social media, printed materials, even a conference badge.

We design brand identities as systems, not isolated assets. A logo, a colour palette, a type system, an illustration style, a tone of voice — all of these are designed to work together and to be applied by the team without needing a designer for every small decision.

The brand also needs to convert. For startups, "looking good" is necessary but not sufficient. The brand needs to communicate the value proposition quickly, build trust with sceptical audiences, and create enough visual distinction to be remembered. Every choice — from the typeface to the colour temperature — is made with conversion in mind.

## Startup website design

The website is the startup's public face. For many early-stage companies, it is the product — or at least the first step toward the product. A visitor lands on the homepage, and within seconds they decide whether to stay, sign up, book a demo, or leave.

We approach startup website design as a conversion-focused exercise. This means starting with the user's journey: what brought them here, what do they need to understand, and what action do we want them to take?

The structure follows from the story. The hero section communicates the value proposition. The next section builds credibility — testimonials, logos, metrics, or a clear explanation of how the product works. The page ends with a clear call to action.

Visual design supports the narrative. Layout, typography, imagery, and animation all serve the story. Nothing is decorative for its own sake. Every element earns its place by advancing the user toward the desired action.

## Product and interface design

For startups building digital products, the interface is the product. The design of the user experience — the flows, the interactions, the information architecture — determines whether the product is useful, usable, and delightful.

We design interfaces grounded in user research and business objectives. This means starting with user flows, mapping out the key journeys, identifying friction points, and prototyping solutions before investing in high-fidelity design.

The visual design of the interface inherits the brand system. The same colours, typography, and visual language that appear on the website and the pitch deck appear in the product. This creates coherence across every touchpoint and reduces the cognitive load for users moving between them.

## Pitch decks and investor materials

The pitch deck is where the brand narrative meets the fundraising story. The same strategic work that informs the brand identity and the website copy feeds directly into the deck.

This is why we treat all deliverables as parts of one system. The pitch deck is not a separate project with a separate visual language. It is an extension of the brand, using the same design system, the same narrative structure, and the same visual identity. When an investor sees the deck and then visits the website, the experience is seamless.

We cover this in more detail in our post on [startup pitch deck design](/blog/startup-pitch-deck-design).

## Designed to convert, designed to adapt

The common thread across all of these deliverables — branding, websites, product interfaces, pitch decks — is that they are designed to convert. To turn a visitor into a user, an investor into a backer, a prospect into a customer.

But they are also designed to adapt. Startups change direction. The product evolves, the market shifts, the team grows. A good design system accommodates this. It gives the team the tools to apply the brand consistently as the company scales, without needing to redesign from scratch every time something changes.

That is what a design process for startups should produce: not just beautiful artifacts, but a system that works harder than the team can on its own.
